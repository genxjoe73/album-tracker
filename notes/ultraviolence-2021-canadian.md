Released June 13, 2014 by Polydor and Interscope, *Ultraviolence* is Lana Del Rey's third studio album and the deliberate sonic break from *Born to Die*. Where *Born to Die* (2012) was hip-hop-influenced trip-pop with prominent string arrangements and trap-adjacent percussion, *Ultraviolence* is a slow, narcotic, guitar-driven record built around analog production warmth — a near-total inversion of her commercial breakthrough's sound.

The album was produced by **Dan Auerbach** (of The Black Keys) at his Easy Eye Sound studio in Nashville. Auerbach's production approach — recording mostly to tape, building songs around bass and drums with extensive reverb and minimal layering — is responsible for the album's distinctive sonic character: dry vocals over wet rhythm, lots of tremolo guitar, very little contemporary pop polish. Del Rey has said she wrote much of the album in Nashville while collaborating with Auerbach in person rather than the long-distance Email-and-Logic-Pro workflow that produced earlier records.

Critically, *Ultraviolence* is often cited as the moment Del Rey's "haters" lost the argument — the album's sustained ambition and willingness to abandon her commercial signature won over critics who had dismissed *Born to Die* as a calculated industry product. Pitchfork moved her score from a 5.5 (*Born to Die*) to an 8.0 (*Ultraviolence*); the album hit #1 on the US Billboard 200 in its first week.

Track D1 is officially titled *Fucked My Way Up to the Top* — a deliberately confrontational title that responded to the persistent industry narrative that Del Rey's career was somehow inauthentic. Most pressings censor it on the labels and inserts.

**About this 2021 Canadian reissue (2×LP):** Polydor/Interscope catalog `B0020950-01`, **Made in Canada** (printed on the rear shrinkwrap). Critically, this is **not** a straight repress of the 2014 lacquers — it's a fresh re-cut by **Scott Hull at Masterdisk** (the `MASTERDISK SH` stamp in the runout, with cut codes `285541E1` through `E4` for the four sides plus the `024xxxxx` / `243xxxx` Universal job-tracking numbers).

Masterdisk is one of the highest-regarded mastering and cutting facilities in the US, and Scott Hull is a well-known cutter (his credits include Steely Dan reissues, Frank Sinatra remasters, and most of Mobile Fidelity's catalog work). The 2021 Masterdisk cut is therefore a deliberate audiophile-tier re-cut from the original masters rather than a mechanical reproduction of the 2014 lacquers. Whether it sounds "better" or "different" than the 2014 cut is a matter of taste — Hull's cuts are typically slightly more open and less compressed than the originals — but it is meaningfully a different vinyl experience from the original first press.

This is the most accessible current-press option for *Ultraviolence* on vinyl, with the additional benefit of being an audiophile-grade re-cut.
