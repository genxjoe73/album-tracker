**Pink Floyd at Pompeii** stands as one of the most iconic and consequential live rock performances ever captured on film. The concert was recorded in August 1971 at the ancient Roman amphitheater in Pompeii, Italy — a location chosen for its visual and acoustic properties, with the band performing to an empty audience save for the film crew, making it a live-to-film performance rather than a traditional concert. The original film, released in 1974 on VHS and later as a theatrical release, became a touchstone artifact of 1970s rock cinema, capturing Pink Floyd at the absolute peak of their *Dark Side of the Moon* era, just months before that album's November 1973 release.

The Pompeii performance is distinguished by its setlist — primarily material from *Dark Side of the Moon* in its pre-release form (the album was still in development when the performance was recorded), alongside selections from earlier albums and extended instrumental passages. The sound — recorded on multitrack tape — was later mixed for the original film release, creating a sonic document of extraordinary clarity and spatial presence that complemented the visual grandeur of the venue. For decades, this remains the definitive live document of Pink Floyd in their experimental studio-as-instrument phase.

The **2025 reissue**, titled **Pink Floyd at Pompeii MCMLXXII** (using Roman numerals for 1972, though the concert was recorded in 1971), represents a comprehensive restoration and remastering project. The film was remastered to **4K resolution** with a fresh **2025 stereo mix** overseen by **Nigel Godrich** — one of contemporary rock's most respected mix engineers, known for his work with Radiohead and others. A separate **Dolby Atmos mix** was also prepared for immersive playback systems. The **Blu-ray** format allows for high-bitrate delivery of both video and audio, preserving the dynamic and spatial ambitions of the original performance and the contemporary restoration work.

This 2025 edition is the first major reissue of Pompeii in the streaming and physical media era, making it newly accessible to listeners who may not have encountered the original theatrical or VHS release.
