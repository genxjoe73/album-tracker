Released October/November 1971, *Meddle* is the album where Pink Floyd figured out their next decade. Coming off *Ummagumma*'s solo-segment experiments and *Atom Heart Mother*'s orchestral-suite ambitions, the band needed to consolidate — and *Meddle* is the document of that consolidation.

The breakthrough is *Echoes*, the 23-minute side-2 piece that became the prototype for everything from *Dark Side of the Moon* through *Wish You Were Here*. The band built it from a year of recorded fragments labeled "Nothing, Pts. 1-24" — keyboard sketches, guitar phrases, sound effects — that they assembled at Abbey Road, AIR Studios, and Morgan Sound through early 1971. Richard Wright's submarine-ping piano (the famous opening note, processed through a Leslie cabinet) was an accident: he hit a single note while testing the equipment and the band kept it. *Echoes* is the first time Floyd's signature dynamic-and-spatial language fully cohered.

Side 1 is openly transitional. *One of These Days* (Roger Waters' twin-bass instrumental with Nick Mason's vocoder-spoken "I am about to cut you into little pieces") points forward; *San Tropez* and *Fearless* are pleasant but conventional rock songs the band was unsure about; *Seamus* features David Gilmour's actual dog Seamus howling along to a 12-bar blues — recorded for real, the dog was at the studio with Steve Marriott (who was minding him for a tour), and Gilmour played him an open tuning until he started singing. The band has since called *Seamus* a mistake; Roger Waters said in 1972 that they thought it was funny at the time.

**About this US Capitol pressing:** Capitol issued *Meddle* as `SMAS-832` from late 1971 onward. Multiple plants pressed it across the early-to-mid 1970s — your "early/mid-1970s" frame matches a 1974 repress or a 1975 reissue depending on the deadwax stamps. The captured ID (r7651494) is the 1975 reissue with documented etched runouts (`Smas-1-832 H-35 Jay ─◁` / `SMAS-2-832 G44#2 ─◁`); the `─◁` triangle is Capitol's mastering mark and the letter-number combos identify the cutting engineer and cut number.
