Revolver was recorded between April and June 1966 at Abbey Road Studios, with George Martin producing and Geoff Emerick — then just nineteen years old — serving as chief engineer. The album represents the sharpest single-album leap in the Beatles' catalog: where Beatles For Sale and Help! had still drawn heavily on touring-band energy and American R&B influences, Revolver was almost entirely a studio artifact, conceived and realized in ways that could not be reproduced on a stage. The Beatles had announced their retirement from live performance mid-production, and the freedom that came with that decision is audible throughout. **Automatic Double Tracking (ADT)**, invented by Emerick and technical engineer Ken Townsend specifically for this project, allowed Lennon to double his vocals without singing twice — the slight phase drift between the two tape passes creates the blurred, slightly uneasy quality that runs through "Tomorrow Never Knows" and "I'm Only Sleeping." That closing track also features tape loops assembled by the band members at home and played back simultaneously on multiple machines in the studio, giving the track its smeared, cyclical texture. Backwards guitar appears on "I'm Only Sleeping," and Harrison's immersion in Indian classical music surfaces throughout "Love You To" with sitar and tabla integrated structurally rather than decoratively. "Eleanor Rigby" dispensed with the band almost entirely, built around a George Martin double-string-quartet arrangement. By the time the album was finished, the Beatles had effectively made live performance obsolete as a creative constraint, and everything that followed — Sgt. Pepper's, the White Album, Abbey Road — flows directly from what was worked out here.

The 2022 reissue is built around a new stereo remix produced by **Giles Martin**, son of George Martin, in collaboration with Sam Okell. The remix draws on the original four-track and eight-track session tapes, but its most significant technical development is the use of **MAL (Machine Auditory Learning)**, a stem-separation tool developed by WingNut Films' audio team for Peter Jackson's Get Back documentary. MAL uses machine-learning analysis to isolate individual instruments and voices from combined recordings that were never intended to be separated — a necessary approach given that Revolver was tracked in an era when instruments were routinely bounced together to a single track to free up tape channels. The remix clarifies the low end of "Got to Get You into My Life," gives "Here, There and Everywhere" a more intimate vocal presence, and opens up the stereo field of "Eleanor Rigby" in ways the original narrow-spread 1966 stereo could not accommodate. Collectors have responded with genuine division. Those who regard the original mono mix — overseen by the Beatles themselves and George Martin — as the definitive text find the new version too clean and too spatially resolved. Others note that the remix reveals detail in the ensemble performances that the dense original bounces had buried. Both positions have merit. The 2022 remix is best understood as a distinct creative document rather than a correction of the original.

**Half-speed mastering** is a lacquer-cutting technique in which the record lathe runs at exactly half of its playback speed — 16⅔ RPM for a standard 33⅓ RPM LP — while the source audio plays back at that same reduced speed. Because the cutting stylus is traveling through the lacquer at half pace, it has twice as much time to trace high-frequency information into the groove wall. This matters because HF content is where conventional mastering most often loses resolution: the cutting stylus physically cannot respond quickly enough to reproduce very high frequencies at full cutting speed without distortion or attenuation. Running the lathe at half speed effectively doubles the stylus's available response time, yielding better HF definition, tighter transient reproduction, and a wider and more stable stereo image. **Miles Showell** has been Abbey Road's primary half-speed practitioner for over a decade and is among the most experienced cutters working in this format. His approach tends toward accuracy and restraint — not a heavy EQ cutter — and his half-speed work on Abbey Road's catalogue reissues has a consistent quality of openness in the high midrange and a well-controlled low end. The deadwax inscriptions on this pressing — `Miles. ABBEY ROAD 1/2 SPEED. ROOM 30` on both sides — confirm that Showell cut these lacquers himself in Room 30, Abbey Road's dedicated mastering suite.
