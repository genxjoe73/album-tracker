Look Up Child

Production notes

Released September 7, 2018 by Centricity Music, Look Up Child is Lauren Daigle’s third studio album and the record that broke her into mainstream pop visibility. You Say, the album’s lead single, became the longest-running number-one on the Billboard Hot Christian Songs chart in the chart’s history (132 weeks) and crossed over to mainstream pop radio — a rare crossover for a contemporary Christian release.

The album was produced by Jason Ingram and Paul Mabury (the same team behind Daigle’s earlier How Can It Be). Recorded primarily in Nashville, the production sits at the intersection of contemporary Christian, adult-pop, and Americana — Daigle’s voice (a husky alto often compared to Adele’s) is consistently the centerpiece, with arrangements built around it rather than competing with it.

The album won Best Contemporary Christian Music Album at the 2019 Grammys and was certified Platinum by the RIAA, with You Say certified 4× Platinum. Look Up Child has been the commercial high-water mark of contemporary Christian music in the post-streaming era — Daigle’s success on this record opened doors for Christian artists to reach mainstream pop audiences without re-recording or re-releasing material in a “secular” form.

About this 2018 US pressing (2×LP, 45 RPM, 180g): Centricity Music issued this on 45 RPM — an audiophile-tier choice. 45 RPM cuts use roughly twice the linear groove length per minute compared to standard 33⅓, which means wider grooves, more headroom for dynamic range, and lower distortion. The trade-off is shorter playable time per side, hence the 2×LP format. Combined with 180g vinyl weight (better surface stability, less warp risk over time), this is a deliberate audiophile-grade pressing rather than a budget reissue.

The vinyl release was delayed from the album’s September 2018 launch until December 2018 — the gap was production capacity, not a problem with the cut. The package includes a gatefold cover and separate lyric inserts for sides A/B and C/D. Song titles are deliberately omitted from the vinyl labels themselves (only the album title, artist, and side letter appear) — a stylistic choice, common on prestige pressings.
