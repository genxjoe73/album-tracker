Production notes

By April 1973, the Beatles had been dissolved as a legal and commercial entity for nearly three years, their partnership formally wound up in the courts while their catalog continued to sell in staggering quantities. EMI and Capitol Records recognized what was obvious to any accountant: there was enormous appetite for a definitive retrospective. The result was 1962–1966, the so-called Red Album, released on April 2, 1973, on Apple Records — the Beatles’ own label — covering the band’s early period from their first single through Revolver and capturing the transition from raw Merseybeat to the increasingly ambitious studio craft that would define their later work. The Red Album was compiled and sequenced to tell a coherent story: four boys from Liverpool who learned their trade in Hamburg and the Cavern Club, broke globally in 1964, and spent the following two years dismantling the very commercial formula that had made them famous. It was the first official double-album retrospective the band had authorized, and its appearance alongside the simultaneously released Blue Album (1967–1970) established the template for how the catalog would be managed and presented for decades.

The original 1973 pressing carried the Apple Records label, but Capitol — which had held US distribution rights since the beginning — continued pressing the compilation into the mid-1970s under its own label identity. This pressing is one of those Capitol-label represses. The catalog number SKBO-3403 is consistent across both issues; the label change reflects Capitol’s ongoing distribution relationship rather than any change to the underlying source material. Capitol’s involvement in the US market had always complicated the Beatles’ discography, most infamously through the label’s practice of resequencing UK albums and adding tracks, creating an alternate American canon that ran parallel to the EMI originals until the catalog was globally standardized in 1987.

For the physical manufacture of this pressing, Capitol contracted Presswell, a New Jersey-based pressing plant operating out of facilities in Totowa and Ancora. Presswell was one of the busiest contract pressing operations of the 1970s and handled work for multiple major labels. Its pressings are identifiable in deadwax by the (PR) designation, which appears consistently across all four sides of this copy. Presswell product is generally regarded as competent commercial work — not in the same conversation as the early UK Parlophone pressings cut at EMI’s Abbey Road, but reliable and well-suited to the high-volume, wide-distribution mandate Capitol had for this title.

Mastering for this pressing was done at Capitol’s own facility, as indicated by the MASTERED BY CAPITOL inscription in all four deadwax grooves. The hand-etched name Gene on Side 1 identifies the Capitol mastering engineer responsible for that lacquer cut — a common practice at Capitol, where engineers sometimes initialed or signed their work by hand in the lacquer. The G-series lacquer matrix numbers (G-33, G-37, G-35, G-36 across the four sides) place this in mid-to-late run Capitol cutting activity, consistent with a mid-1970s repress timeline rather than the initial 1973 Apple pressing campaign.
