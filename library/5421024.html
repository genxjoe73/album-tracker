Production notes

Where the Red Album documented ascent, 1967–1970 — the Blue Album — documents a different kind of story: the Beatles at the absolute peak of their studio ambition, and then the slow, painful unraveling of the band that produced that work. The period covered begins with Sgt. Pepper’s Lonely Hearts Club Band and runs through the psychedelic experiments of Magical Mystery Tour and the White Album, the refined classicism of Abbey Road, and finally the fractured, contentious sessions that produced Let It Be — released posthumously in May 1970 after the band had already, for all practical purposes, ceased to exist. Released on April 2, 1973, simultaneously with the Red Album, the Blue Album was conceived as the second half of a matched set: the two compilations together spanning the complete arc of the Beatles’ recording career.

The 1973 Apple Records pressing carries the blue Apple label on vinyl — the label variant Apple used for Beatles releases during this period, as distinct from the green Apple used for non-Beatles Apple Records artists. Apple Records itself was the Beatles’ own label, founded in 1968 as part of a broader corporate venture into entertainment and retail. The blue label on this pressing is original to the 1973 release and distinguishes it from the Capitol-label represses that followed in the mid-1970s. The gatefold sleeve for both compilations was a deliberate presentation choice, providing room for the iconic photographs — the complementary rooftop images that gave both records their visual identity and connected them visually across the matched set.

Despite the Apple Records label on the sleeve and vinyl, the actual mastering work for the US pressing was done at Capitol’s facilities, and the deadwax codes reflect this. The notation CCD on Side 2 identifies Capitol Custom Disc, Capitol’s Hollywood mastering division, which handled custom and compilation work for the label and its affiliates. CCD’s involvement here is consistent with the compilation’s status as a major release requiring dedicated mastering attention rather than routine production.

The F- and G-series lacquer matrix numbers across the four sides (F40, G42, G11, F48) place this pressing in mid-run Capitol cutting activity. The earliest lacquers for any given Capitol title tend to carry the highest-letter designations — H, Z — representing the first cuts from the source tape. F and G cuts are later in sequence, indicating this pressing was manufactured after the initial pressing campaign had consumed the earliest lacquers. This is consistent with a mid-to-late 1973 date, plausibly later in the year rather than from the very first April pressing run. The +8 notation on Side 4 is a stamper designation indicating the generation number of the stamper used for that side.

About this 1973 US Apple Records pressing (2×LP, gatefold): Apple Records catalog SKBO-3404, blue Apple label on vinyl, gatefold sleeve. Deadwax: Side 1 SKBO-1-3404 F40 / Side 2 SKBO-2-3404 G42 CCD / Side 3 SKBO-3-3404 G11 / Side 4 SKBO-4-3404 F48 +8. CCD on Side 2 = Capitol Custom Disc (Capitol Hollywood mastering). F/G lacquer series = mid-run cutting, consistent with mid-to-late 1973. The +8 on Side 4 is a stamper notation. Note: this exact deadwax combination does not appear as a standalone Discogs entry and is captured against r5421024 as the closest documented variant.
