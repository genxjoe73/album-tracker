Production notes

Chronicle: The 20 Greatest Hits is a compilation by Creedence Clearwater Revival released in January 1976 by Fantasy Records. CCR had disbanded in 1972, and by 1976, the catalog had accumulated enough distance and cultural resonance to support a definitive retrospective. The 20-track selection spans their entire run from 1967 to 1970 — “Susie Q” (the John Lee Hooker cover that launched them), “Proud Mary,” “Bad Moon Rising,” “Fortunate Son,” “Green River,” and “Lookin’ Out My Back Door,” among others. Chronicle became one of CCR’s best-selling releases and remains the standard compilation by which the band is known to casual listeners.

Fantasy Records was the independent label that released all of CCR’s studio albums and held the master tapes and publishing rights throughout the band’s career and beyond. The label was founded by Saul Zaentz in the 1950s and built on jazz catalog; CCR’s commercial dominance from 1969 onward transformed Fantasy into a major force in the American recording industry. This 1976 pressing was manufactured and distributed from Fantasy’s headquarters in Berkeley, California, as indicated on the label.

Early pressings of Chronicle carry the ⁀..⁀ symbol in the deadwax, indicating mastering by David Turner, Fantasy’s house mastering engineer. Your copy carries a G# lacquer designation, suggesting a mid-series pressing — later in the initial production run but before the “mixed plates” configuration that appears on later repressings. The random P etch is an individual engineer or QC mark.

About this 1976 US pressing (2×LP, gatefold): Fantasy Records catalog CCR-2, manufactured and distributed from Berkeley, California. Mastered by David Turner (indicated by ⁀..⁀ on early copies; your pressing carries G# lacquer). Deadwax: CCR-2 / G# / P. Gatefold sleeve. No barcode (original 1976 pressing).

Gemini Notes

Released in 1976, Chronicle: The 20 Greatest Hits is the definitive compilation of Creedence Clearwater Revival’s unmatched run of late-60s and early-70s singles. Featuring John Fogerty’s swamp-rock songwriting mastery, it remains one of the best-selling greatest hits albums in music history.
